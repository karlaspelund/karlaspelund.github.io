Research Papers

    Who Gets the Fish? Protecting Firms and Workers from Permit Trade

    Job Market Paper

    Regulators often introduce restrictions on trade in environmental permit markets, lowe ring the gains from permit trade to meet distributional goals like protecting small-scale production or jobs. I study the efficiency and distributional consequences of two common designs—segmenting permit trading by firm characteristics and requirements to produce from a share of the allocations—in one of the world’s oldest and largest fisheries permit markets in Iceland. Using the staggered introduction of permit trading in firm-level difference-in-differences design, I find that the permit market as designed shifted production to more productive boats. Running a similar analysis at the worker level, I find that the significant turnover on fishing boats leads to few earnings impacts on those working in fisheries when trading is introduced, though with earnings falling for those on less productive boats in the post-period. I also document evidence of production distortions from segmenting the permit market and from production requirements. I then develop and estimate a joint model of fishery harvests and permit trading to quantify the cost in foregone profits of re-distributing production via the trading limits. Segmentation costs 0.77 dollars for every dollar increase in harvest profits to small boats and 2.50 dollars for every dollar increase in small-boat earnings. The production requirement is twice as expensive a re-distributive policy.

    Additionality and Asymmetric Information in Environmental Markets: Evidence from Conservation Auctions

    with Anna Russo

    PDF, VoxDev

    Market mechanisms aim to deliver environmental services at low cost. However, this objective is undermined by participants whose conservation actions are not marginal to the incentive — or “additional” — as the lowest cost providers of environmental services may not be the highest social value. We investigate this potential market failure in the world’s largest auction mechanism for ecosystem services, the Conservation Reserve Program, with a dataset linking bids in the program’s scoring auction to satellite-derived land use. We use a regression discontinuity design to show that three of four marginal winners of the auction are not additional. Moreover, we find that the heterogeneity in counterfactual land use introduces adverse selection in the market. We then develop and estimate a joint model of multi-dimensional bidding and land use to quantify the implications of this market failure for the performance of environmental procurement mechanisms and competitive offset markets. We design alternative auctions with scoring rules that incorporate the expected impact of the auction on bidders’ land use. These auctions increase efficiency by using bids and observed characteristics to select participants based on both costs and expected additionality.
